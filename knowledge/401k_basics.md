# 401(k) Basics — Plain English Guide

## What Is a 401(k)?

A retirement savings account through your employer. Money goes in before taxes, grows tax-free, and you pay taxes when you take it out in retirement (when you'll probably be in a lower tax bracket).

## Why It's the Best Deal in Finance

### The Employer Match
If your company matches your contribution, that's literally free money. If they match $1 for $1 up to a certain amount, not contributing at least enough to get the full match is like turning down a raise.

Example: You put in $100/week, company puts in $100/week = $200/week going into your account but only $100 came from your paycheck.

### The Tax Break
Every dollar you put in reduces your taxable income. If you're in the 12% bracket, a $100 contribution only costs you $88 in take-home pay. The other $12 was going to the IRS anyway.

### Tax-Deferred Growth
Your investments grow without being taxed every year. In a regular account, you'd pay taxes on dividends and gains annually. In a 401(k), that money stays invested and compounds.

## Traditional vs Roth 401(k)

| | Traditional 401(k) | Roth 401(k) |
|--|-------------------|-------------|
| Tax on contributions | No (pre-tax) | Yes (after-tax) |
| Tax on withdrawals | Yes (taxed as income) | No (tax-free) |
| Best if | In higher bracket now than retirement | In lower bracket now than retirement |
| Reduces current taxable income | Yes | No |

Most people in the 12% bracket: Traditional is usually better because you're already in a low bracket. But Roth has value if you expect much higher income later.

## The Power of Compound Growth

Starting with $0, contributing $200/week at 7% average return:

| Years | Total Contributed | Account Value | Free Money (Growth) |
|-------|------------------|---------------|-------------------|
| 5 | $52,000 | $62,400 | $10,400 |
| 10 | $104,000 | $153,000 | $49,000 |
| 15 | $156,000 | $280,000 | $124,000 |
| 20 | $208,000 | $468,000 | $260,000 |
| 25 | $260,000 | $740,000 | $480,000 |
| 30 | $312,000 | $1,095,000 | $783,000 |

After 30 years, **71% of your balance is growth, not contributions.** Time is the cheat code.

## When Can You Take It Out?

- **Age 59½**: Penalty-free withdrawals, taxed as regular income
- **Before 59½**: 10% early withdrawal penalty + income taxes (almost never worth it)
- **Hardship withdrawal**: Medical emergencies, preventing eviction — still taxed + penalized
- **Loan from 401(k)**: Some plans allow borrowing from yourself — you pay yourself back with interest, but if you leave the job you usually have to repay immediately

## Common Mistakes

### Not contributing enough to get the full match
This is leaving free money on the table. If the match is $100/week, contribute at least $100/week. Period.

### Cashing out when changing jobs
If you leave a job, ROLL the 401(k) into an IRA or your new employer's plan. Don't cash it out — you'll pay taxes + 10% penalty and destroy years of growth.

### Not increasing contributions with raises
Every time you get a raise, increase your 401(k) by at least half the raise amount. You'll never miss money you never saw in your check.

### Being too conservative
If you're under 45, your 401(k) should be mostly in stock index funds (like an S&P 500 fund), not bonds or "stable value" funds. You have decades for the market to recover from dips. Being too safe costs you hundreds of thousands in growth over 30 years.

## The One-Page Summary
1. Contribute at least enough to get the full employer match
2. Increase contributions every time you get a raise
3. Don't touch it until retirement
4. Put it in stock index funds if you're under 45
5. Every dollar you put in costs you less than a dollar (tax savings)
6. Time + compound growth = millionaire math on a regular salary
