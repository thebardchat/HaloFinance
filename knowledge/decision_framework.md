# Decision Framework: Easier, Faster, Biggest Return

## The Philosophy

Every financial decision should be filtered through three questions, in this order:

1. **Biggest Return** — Which option puts the most money in my pocket?
2. **Easier** — Which option has fewer steps, less friction, less confusion?
3. **Faster** — Which option gets results sooner?

If one option clearly wins on all three, that's your answer. No analysis paralysis needed.

## Why This Order Matters

- **Biggest return first** because saving $50 on fees while losing $2,000 in refund is a $1,950 mistake
- **Easier second** because complexity causes inaction, and inaction costs money
- **Faster third** because money today is worth more than money next year (time value)

## How to Apply It

### Step 1: Identify Your Options
Don't list 10 options. List 2-3 realistic ones.

### Step 2: Run the Numbers Side by Side

| | Option A | Option B |
|--|----------|----------|
| Gross benefit | $X | $Y |
| Costs (fees, time, complexity) | ($X) | ($Y) |
| **Net to you** | **$X** | **$Y** |
| Steps required | X steps | Y steps |
| Time to result | X weeks | Y weeks |

### Step 3: Pick the Winner
- If one option has a clearly higher net return, that's the winner
- If returns are close (within $100), pick the easier/faster one
- If you're stuck between equal options, pick the one you can start RIGHT NOW

### Step 4: Execute
Don't second-guess. The cost of delay almost always exceeds the cost of picking the "wrong" equal option.

## Common Applications

### Tax Software
Don't pick the cheapest. Pick the one that gives the biggest refund, then subtract the cost.
- A $200 program that gets $5,000 back = $4,800 net
- A $15 program that gets $2,500 back = $2,485 net
- The "expensive" option is $2,315 cheaper

### Debt Payoff Strategy
Don't overthink avalanche vs. snowball. If your interest rates are all similar (25-35%), the mathematical difference is small. Pick the method you'll actually stick with.
- The "best" strategy you abandon after 2 months = $0 saved
- The "good enough" strategy you follow for 18 months = debt free

### Insurance
Don't just find the cheapest premium. Compare total cost of ownership:
- Premium + deductible + out-of-pocket max = what you could actually pay
- A $100/mo cheaper plan with a $5,000 higher deductible isn't cheaper if you use it

### Retirement Contributions
Don't skip the employer match. Ever.
- Not contributing enough to get the full match is turning down free money
- "I can't afford $100/week" — but you can afford to lose $100/week of free money?

### Emergency Fund vs. Debt Payoff
Don't pay off 28% credit cards while keeping $10,000 in a 4% savings account.
BUT also don't drain your emergency fund to $0 — one car repair puts you back in debt.
- Keep $1,000-1,500 minimum emergency fund
- Everything else attacks debt
- Rebuild the full emergency fund after consumer debt is gone

## Red Flags: When You're Overcomplicating It

- Spending 3 hours comparing options that differ by $20
- Reading 10 blog posts instead of making a decision
- Waiting for "perfect information" before acting
- Switching strategies every month instead of sticking with one
- Optimizing pennies while ignoring dollars

## The Anti-Pattern: "Penny Wise, Dollar Foolish"

| Decision | Penny-Wise | Dollar-Smart |
|----------|------------|--------------|
| Tax software | "I'll save $185 with the free option" | "I'll make $2,500 more with the paid option" |
| Driving across town for cheaper gas | Save $3 on gas | Spend $5 in gas + 30 minutes |
| DIY vs. professional | Save $200 on tax prep | Miss $3,000 in deductions |
| Skipping 401(k) to pay bills | $100 more this week | Lose $100 employer match forever |
| Cheapest insurance | Save $50/month | Pay $5,000 more when you need it |

## The Default Rule

**If what you're doing is working, keep doing it.**

Only switch when the alternative clearly wins on return AND doesn't add meaningful friction. The grass isn't always greener — sometimes the cost of switching (time, confusion, risk) eats the theoretical savings.

## For Financial Advisors / AI Assistants

When presenting options to someone:
1. **Lead with the recommendation**, not a menu of choices
2. **Show the math** — side-by-side comparison, net of all costs
3. **Factor in complexity** — a slightly worse option that's 10x simpler may be the right call
4. **Default to what's working** — don't fix what isn't broken
5. **Make it actionable** — "Do X" beats "You could consider X or Y or Z"
