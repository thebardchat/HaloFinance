# 2025 Federal Tax Brackets — Plain English Guide

## How Tax Brackets Actually Work

Tax brackets are like buckets that fill up in order. You don't pay one rate on all your money — you pay each rate only on the money that falls in that bucket.

Think of it like a swimming pool with sections:
- The first section of water (income) gets taxed at the lowest rate
- Only the water that spills into the next section gets taxed at the higher rate
- You NEVER lose money by earning more

## Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) — 2025

| Bucket | Taxable Income | Rate | Max Tax in This Bucket |
|--------|---------------|------|----------------------|
| 1st | $0 — $23,850 | 10% | $2,385 |
| 2nd | $23,851 — $96,950 | 12% | $8,772 |
| 3rd | $96,951 — $206,700 | 22% | $24,145 |
| 4th | $206,701 — $394,600 | 32% | $60,128 |
| 5th | $394,601 — $501,050 | 35% | $37,257 |
| 6th | $501,051+ | 37% | no cap |

Remember: "Taxable income" = gross wages minus pre-tax deductions (401k, insurance) minus standard deduction ($30,000 MFJ).

## Common Myths — BUSTED

### "If I make more I'll lose money because of taxes"
**FALSE.** If you earn $1 over a bracket line, only that $1 gets taxed at the higher rate. The rest stays at the lower rates. You always take home more by earning more.

### "I should turn down overtime to stay in a lower bracket"
**FALSE.** This is the most expensive myth in America. Even if OT pushes you into the 22% bracket, you keep 78 cents of every OT dollar in that bracket. That's still $32.18/hr take-home on a $41.25 OT rate. You'd turn down $32/hr because it's not $33? No.

### "My whole paycheck gets taxed at my bracket rate"
**FALSE.** If you're "in the 22% bracket," that doesn't mean 22% of your income goes to taxes. It means only the portion ABOVE $96,950 (MFJ) is taxed at 22%. Everything below is still taxed at 10% and 12%.

### "A big raise will put me in a higher bracket and I'll bring home less"
**FALSE.** A raise from $90K to $100K taxable (MFJ) means $6,950 is taxed at 12% and $3,050 at 22%. Your tax goes up by $1,505 on $10,000 of new income. You still take home $8,495 more. Always.

## How to Reduce Taxable Income (Legally)
1. **401(k) contributions** — every dollar reduces taxable income dollar-for-dollar
2. **HSA contributions** — if eligible, $8,300 family limit (2025)
3. **Traditional IRA** — $7,000/person if income qualifies
4. **Pre-tax insurance premiums** — already happening through payroll
5. **Standard deduction** — automatic $30,000 for MFJ

## Tax Credits vs Deductions — What's the Difference?
- **Deduction**: reduces your taxable income. $1,000 deduction at 12% bracket saves you $120.
- **Credit**: reduces your actual tax bill dollar for dollar. $1,000 credit saves you $1,000. Credits are way more powerful.

Key credits for families:
- Child Tax Credit: $2,000 per kid under 17
- Other Dependent Credit: $500 per qualifying relative
- Earned Income Tax Credit: phases out above certain income
- Child and Dependent Care Credit: if you pay for childcare
