# W-4 Explained — Plain English Guide

## What Is the W-4?

A form you give your employer that tells them how much federal tax to take out of every paycheck. It doesn't change how much tax you actually owe — it just controls how much gets prepaid throughout the year.

Think of it like this: your annual tax bill is a fixed amount. The W-4 decides whether you pay it in small weekly chunks (withholding) or in one big payment in April (owing at tax time), or whether you massively overpay all year and get a big refund.

## The Big Myth: "Claiming More = Paying Less Tax"

**Wrong.** Claiming dependents on the W-4 does NOT change your tax bill. It changes your withholding — how much comes out of each check. Your actual tax is calculated when you file your return. The W-4 just controls the prepayment schedule.

## Why Your Refund Might Be Too Big

A big refund means you overpaid all year. The IRS held your money — interest-free — and gave it back months later. A $10,000 refund means you loaned the government ~$192/week for free.

The ideal refund is $0 to $1,000. Enough to cover rounding errors, small enough that you had your money all year.

## How to Fill Out the 2020+ W-4 (Step by Step)

### Step 1: Personal Information
- Your name, address, SSN
- **Filing status**: Check one box
  - **Single**: unmarried, no dependents
  - **Married Filing Jointly**: married, filing together (wider brackets = less withheld)
  - **Head of Household**: unmarried with qualifying dependents

### Step 2: Multiple Jobs or Spouse Works
- **Only fill this out if**: you have two jobs, OR you're married and BOTH spouses work
- **Leave blank if**: you're the only earner in the household
- Checking the box here increases withholding. If only one spouse works, checking it will over-withhold.

### Step 3: Claim Dependents
This is the most important line for families. This tells your employer about tax credits you'll get so they can reduce withholding accordingly.

- **Qualifying children under 17**: count them × $2,000
- **Other dependents** (elderly parent, etc.): count them × $500
- Write the total

Example: 3 kids + 1 dependent parent = (3 × $2,000) + (1 × $500) = **$6,500**

If you leave this at $0 when you have kids, your employer withholds as if you have no credits coming. You'll massively overpay all year and get a huge refund. Fix this.

### Step 4: Other Adjustments (Optional)
- **(a) Other income**: Non-job income (investments, rental, etc.). Increases withholding.
- **(b) Deductions**: ONLY if you itemize deductions above the standard deduction. Most people leave this blank.
- **(c) Extra withholding**: A specific dollar amount to withhold extra per pay period. This is your safety valve — add $10-20/week if you want a guaranteed small refund.

### Step 5: Sign and Date

## Common Scenarios

### Single, No Kids, One Job
- Step 1: Single
- Steps 2-4: Leave blank
- Result: Standard withholding, should be close to correct

### Married, Kids, One Earner
- Step 1: Married Filing Jointly
- Step 2: Leave blank
- Step 3: (number of kids × $2,000) + (other dependents × $500)
- Step 4(c): $10-20/week for safety cushion
- Result: Much less withheld, bigger paychecks, small refund

### Married, Kids, Both Spouses Work
- Step 1: Married Filing Jointly
- Step 2: Check the box (or use the worksheet)
- Step 3: Only ONE spouse claims the kids (not both!)
- Step 4(c): $20-40/week for safety
- Result: Balanced withholding across both jobs

### Want a Bigger Refund on Purpose
Some people like the forced savings of a big refund. If that's you:
- Don't claim dependents in Step 3 (leave at $0)
- Add extra in Step 4(c)
- But know that you're choosing to loan the government money at 0% interest

## When to Update Your W-4
- Got married or divorced
- Had a baby
- Spouse started or stopped working
- Got a big raise
- Started a side business
- Dependent status changed (kid turned 17, parent moved in/out)
- Your refund was way too big or you owed a lot

## The Golden Rule
Your goal is to owe $0 and get a refund of $0-$1,000. That means your paychecks are as fat as possible without a surprise tax bill in April. Use Step 3 for credits you know you'll get, and Step 4(c) as a small safety net.
